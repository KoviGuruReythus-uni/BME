## Types of reasoning
- Different scenarios require different approaches to reasoning
- There are 2 
	- Deductive
	- Inductive
- Logic as a discipline provides different methods for assessing the quality of different types of reasoning
	- _Not all reasoning is the same and hence we need different methods to assess them as well. There is no one-size-fits all solution._

### Deductive Reasoning
- There are a couple of premises and then a conclusion AND,
	- The premises undoubtedly justify the conclusion
- The relationship between the premises and the conclusion is the strongest

EXAMPLE: 
$P_1$ - All humans suck
$P_2$ - I am a human
$C$ - I suck 

- _A reasoning that is based on a definition is a deductive reasoning_

- The connection between the premises and the conclusion is called the _scheme of the argument_
	- There are good and bad schemes
- Scheme says nothing about the conclusion's validity or truth, it is simply a means of measurement for the connection between the premises and the conclusion. 

#### Scheme
- Deductive arguments are built on schemes
- You can assess the argument without knowing the exact premises
- Just based on the SCHEME you can tell if the argument is valid or not. 

##### _Examples_
1. if it rains, then the pavement is wet. It is raining now. Therefore, the pavement is wet.
2. If we have an unstable global political situation, then the price of gold goes up. At the end of the 1980s, at the time of the conflict in Afghanistan, we had an unstable global political situation. Therefore, we could see that the price of gold went up

_WHHAT FORMAT TO THESE EXAMPLES HAVE?_
```
if p, then q
p (has happened)
=> q
```

$P_1$ - if $p$ then $q$
$P_2$ - $p$
$C$ - $q$

#### Validity
- a deductive reasoning is valid iff it fits in a valid scheme 
- Valid scheme $\implies$ Valid deductive reasoning

- You can fit (place in) any proposition in a valid scheme and the reasoning will be valid
	- The idea is that it is interchangeable, it is like a template where only the propositions are changed

##### When is a scheme valid?
- It is valid iff we CANNOT find an argument in which the premises are true but the conclusion is false
	- This is a counterexample (for a scheme to be valid, there shouldn't be any counterexamples)
- _In a valid deductive reasoning, the truth of the premises necessitates the truth of the conclusion_ (formal)

##### Soundness
- If we know that the premises are true and the scheme is a valid one, then we say that the argument is _sound_

##### Validity & Soundness
- an argument can be valid but not sound. 
- But ALL sound arguments will have to be valid

###### Examples

_Valid but not sound_
- All bats are birds
- all birds lay eggs
- therefore, bats lay eggs
	- The argument is valid. It follows a valid scheme but it is not sound. Because,  bats are not birds. 
_Invalid (not sound)_
- All cokes are fizzy 
- all champagnes are fizzy 
- therefore, all cokes are champagnes
	- The argument is wrong, it has an invalid scheme. The premises do NOT justify the conclusion

## Inductive Reasoning
- the connection between the premises and the conclusion is not as strong as deductive reasoning. 
	- Therefore, a true premise doesnt always imply a true conclusion

#### Strength
_the connection between the premise and the conclusion_
- Strong - We _can_ presume the conclusion
- Weak - we _cannot_ presume the conclusion
